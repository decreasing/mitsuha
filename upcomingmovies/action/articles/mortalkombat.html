Mortal Kombat2021
GENRE: action adventure fantasy

Video-game adaptations are notoriously hard to pull off, but the task becomes significantly harder when the source material is an arcade fighting game with the plot and characters there just as an excuse to make people fight. After a 1995 adaptation that delivered lukewarm results and its terrible sequel that killed the franchise, 2021’s Mortal Kombat will make the fans of the long-running video game series happy because it turns out the best way to adapt a Mortal Kombat game is to … treat it like a Mortal Kombat game. You consider plot and character development mostly as an afterthought and excel at the only thing gamers love about the series - gory, inventive fatalities.

Mortal Kombat follows a young and struggling boxer named Cole Young who has always had a birthmark he never thought too much of. After a visit from a fighter named Jax, he is told that what he thought was a birthmark is actually a branding that is only given to a few people chosen as fighters who have to defend Earth from invading forces of the realm of Outworld in a once in a generation championship known as Mortal Kombat. As he teams up with Sonya Blade, Kano, Liu Kang, Kung Lao and Raiden to learn the skills necessary to defeat their opponents, the roster of fighters realise that their roster of otherworldly opponents led by Shang Tsung and Sub-Zero will break the rules of the tournament to conquer Earth once and for all. It’s up to our heroes to find their inner powers before it’s too late, and for Cole Young it may all be connected to his lineage.

Let’s start with the good, and that truly is the iconic fight sequences gamers know and love from the series. Director Simon McQuoid did a fantastic job staging these fight sequences with every bit of excitement they deserve, but what’s truly shocking is just how much like the games it is. It won’t be an understatement to say that Mortal Kombat might be the most shockingly gory studio blockbuster in a long, long time. The fatalities here are incredibly brutal and show some very imaginative fatalities in graphic detail. These unexpected moments are the best part of the adaptation by far and worth the price of admission alone if you care for the source material.

It’s a little disappointing that a lot of the best parts are in the red band trailer, though. The movie also does a solid job at visualising the crazier visual aspects of the game whether it comes to the Netherrealm or Sub-Zero’s icy trickeries, by either embracing the crazy or grounding them in a way that we can relate to in our real world. It’s also a movie that doesn’t take itself too seriously all the time, thanks to the addition of Kano as the foul-mouthed wisecracker who is aware of the absurdity of all of this and makes the proceedings a lot more fun to witness.

Where the movie doesn’t entirely land is the story and the characters. Mortal Kombat has always been known to have a complicated lore and mythology, so no one expected the filmmakers to literally translate that here. But what we do get is surprisingly simplified and dumbed down to the point that it creates apathy within the audience with the basic ‘bad people are coming to take over Earth’ storyline. And whatever exposition we do get is not handled elegantly as characters just spew off chunks of it in stilted dialogue scenes. It also makes the world itself feel smaller than it should, especially because we spend a vast majority of the film in a generic cave in the middle of nowhere and the rest of it in alleys and deserted homes. This same dumbing down also applies to the characters, who either have no backstory or incredibly derivative ones. It’s especially evident with Cole Young who feels like a passive protagonist with a cliche ‘chosen one’ backstory that isn’t helped by Lewis Tan’s wooden performance to go with it. The only exception to this is Scorpion who gets an incredibly effective prologue and Hiroyuki Sanada’s performance really makes you feel for his pain.

Do any of those things matter? Should they matter? That’s a question that everyone needs to ask for themselves. If you’re a Mortal Kombat fan, you’ll have a blast since you’ll fill in the gaps with your pre-existing knowledge of the series and just revel in the violence. But for the average viewer, there will be little reason to care for the stakes here. Ultimately that’s what 2021’s Mortal Kombat comes down to - a solid video game adaptation full of fan service for the gamers (stay for the sequel tease) and a decent action movie for everyone else.
